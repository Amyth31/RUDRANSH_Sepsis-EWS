Rudransh is an AI-powered sepsis prediction system built for Indian Army soldiers in forward field locations — Ladakh, Siachen, Arunachal — where hospital access is absent and every hour decides survival.

Mission
01
Built for soldiers who cannot wait for a hospital.

In forward battle areas, the nearest medical facility can be 200+ km away. Sepsis — if undetected — kills within hours. Rudransh uses only the equipment already present in every Regimental Aid Post — a pulse oximeter, BP cuff, and thermometer — to predict sepsis risk up to 6 hours before clinical onset. This gives the Medical Officer time to initiate antibiotics, stabilize vitals, and arrange CASEVAC before the window closes.

02
Why field sepsis is different from ICU sepsis.

Extreme cold suppresses early immune response, masking classic symptoms. Combat trauma opens direct infection pathways. Hypothermia, altitude hypoxia, and physical exhaustion accelerate progression from infection to septic shock faster than standard conditions. Standard clinical tools require lab infrastructure that does not exist at the forward edge. Rudransh produces risk assessments from vitals alone, in real time.

Impact
Global
Sepsis is the world's leading cause of preventable death.

Over 49 million cases annually, responsible for 11 million deaths — 1 in every 5 deaths worldwide. The majority occur in low-resource settings where early detection infrastructure does not exist.

India
South Asia's highest sepsis burden — largely unmonitored.

India records 11.3 million sepsis cases per year with 3.3 million deaths. Between 30–50% of all ICU admissions develop sepsis, with mortality reaching 50–70% in resource-limited settings — precisely the conditions faced at forward military posts.

Forward Fields
Where the system matters most — and least exists.

At Siachen, Ladakh, and Arunachal, soldiers operate above 5,000m with no ICU within reach. Sepsis from combat trauma or frostbite progresses rapidly. Rudransh gives the MO a 6-hour window to act — using only vitals already being monitored at the RAP. Every hour of delayed treatment increases mortality by approximately 7%.
